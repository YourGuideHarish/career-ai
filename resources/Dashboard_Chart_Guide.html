Section 01
10 Chart Types — Illustrated

Each chart shown with real sample data, axis labels, and the rules that govern it.

Chart 01
Bar / Column Chart
Compare values across categories
Most Used
X-axis → Category (Region) Y-axis → Value (Sales ₹) Sort → Descending by value
Use WhenComparing discrete items — regions, products, teams, months
X-AxisCategory (Region, Product, Person)
Y-AxisMeasure (Revenue, Count, Score)
Golden RuleSort bars by value (desc) unless time-based. Always start Y at zero.
AvoidMore than 12–15 categories. Switch to treemap instead.
VariantsHorizontal bar = better for long labels. Grouped = compare two series.
Chart 02
Line Chart
Show trends over time
Time Series
X-axis → Time (Month) Y-axis → Measure (Revenue ₹) Lines → Max 4–5 series
Use WhenData is continuous over time — trends, growth, seasonality
X-AxisALWAYS Time (Date, Month, Quarter, Year)
Y-AxisMeasure (Revenue, Users, Temperature)
Golden RuleLabel lines directly. Use consistent date intervals. Time always on X.
AvoidCategories without natural order. Single data points.
VariantsMultiple lines for comparison. Dashed line for forecast/target.
Chart 03
Pie / Donut Chart
Show part-to-whole composition
Use Sparingly
Segments → Categories Size → % of total Max → 6 slices
Use WhenShowing how a 100% total breaks into parts. Max 6 slices.
X-AxisN/A — segments = categories
Y-AxisN/A — segment size = % share
Golden RuleAlways show %. Sort largest to smallest. Start at 12 o'clock.
AvoidNever compare two pie charts. Never >6 slices. Hard to read accurately.
VariantsDonut = modern, allows centre KPI. Treemap = better for many categories.
Chart 04
Scatter Plot
Show correlation between two variables
Analytical
X-axis → Independent var (Ad Spend) Y-axis → Dependent var (Revenue) Each dot → One data point
Use WhenExploring relationship between two numeric variables. Finding outliers.
X-AxisIndependent variable (the cause / explanatory factor)
Y-AxisDependent variable (the outcome / effect)
Golden RuleAdd a trendline. Label outliers. Use quadrant lines for 2×2 matrix.
AvoidCategorical data on either axis. Dense scatter without trendline.
VariantsBubble chart = 3rd variable shown as dot size. Connected scatter for time.
Chart 05
Stacked Area Chart
Show trend + composition over time
Time + Mix
X-axis → Time (Quarter) Y-axis → Cumulative measure Areas → Max 4 series
Use WhenShowing trend over time AND how composition shifts — who contributed what.
X-AxisTime (same as line chart)
Y-AxisCumulative or stacked measure
Golden RuleLimit to 3–4 series. Use opacity. Label each band directly.
AvoidHeavy overlap between series. Never 3D area charts.
Variants100% stacked area = shows changing % mix (not volume).
Chart 06
Heatmap / Matrix
Find patterns across two dimensions
Pattern Finder
X-axis → Day of Week Y-axis → Hour of Day Colour → Sales volume
Use WhenComparing across two categorical dimensions at once. Spotting hot/cold zones.
X-AxisCategory A (Day of Week, Product)
Y-AxisCategory B (Hour, Region, Quarter)
Golden RuleAlways include a colour legend. Use sequential scale: light=low, dark=high.
AvoidWhen exact values are critical. Watch for colour-blindness accessibility.
VariantsCorrelation matrix (numeric). Geographic heatmap (map overlay).
Chart 07
KPI Card / Scorecard
Highlight one critical metric at a glance
Always First
TOTAL REVENUE
₹2.4Cr
▲ +12% vs last month
TARGET HIT
84%
▼ Target: ₹2.85Cr
NPS SCORE
72
▲ Target: 70 ✓
OPEN TICKETS
142
▲ +18 since yesterday
Structure → Big number + label + change + status colour
Use WhenTop of any dashboard. The most critical number the audience must see first.
StructureBig number · label · % change · vs target/prior period
StatusGreen = on track · Amber = at risk · Red = off track
Golden RuleA number without context is meaningless. Always show vs something.
AvoidKPI card without comparison metric. Four elements max — nothing more.
ExamplesRevenue · NPS · Conversion Rate · Churn · Open Tickets
Chart 08
Waterfall Chart
Show incremental contributions to a total
Bridge Chart
X-axis → Steps / Components Y-axis → Cumulative value 🟢 Add   🔴 Subtract   ⬜ Total
Use WhenExplaining how you got from Start to End through positive and negative steps.
X-AxisSteps or components (Start → changes → End)
Y-AxisCumulative value
Golden RuleShow Start + End as solid bars. Label each step with its change value.
AvoidMore than 8–10 steps. Not for time trends — use line chart instead.
ExamplesProfit Bridge · Budget vs Actual · Cash Flow · P&L Breakdown
Chart 09
Histogram
Show distribution of a continuous variable
Distribution
X-axis → Value ranges (bins) Y-axis → Frequency / Count Note → Bars MUST touch (continuous)
Use WhenUnderstanding how data is spread. Is it normal? Skewed? Outliers?
X-AxisValue ranges / bins of the continuous variable
Y-AxisCount / Frequency of observations per bin
Golden RuleBars must TOUCH each other (unlike bar chart). Choose bin size carefully.
AvoidDon't confuse with bar chart. Bars touching = continuous. Gaps = discrete.
ExamplesOrder Value Distribution · Age Distribution · Salary Frequency
Chart 10
Grouped / Stacked Bar
Compare categories across two dimensions
2-Dimension
X-axis → Primary category (Region) Y-axis → Measure (Sales) Groups → Secondary category (Product)
Use WhenComparing values across two categorical dimensions simultaneously.
X-AxisPrimary category (Region, Quarter)
Y-AxisMeasure (Revenue, Units)
Golden RuleGrouped = compare individual values. Stacked = compare totals + composition.
AvoidMore than 3–4 groups per cluster. Use consistent colour per series.
ExamplesSales by Region × Product · Revenue by Quarter × Channel
Section 02
The Decision Rulebook

Ask these 8 questions in order. The first match wins.

Question to Ask
Chart to Choose
What am I showing?
Comparison → Bar/Column  |  Trend → Line  |  Composition → Pie/Treemap  |  Relationship → Scatter  |  Distribution → Histogram
Is time involved?
Yes, continuous → Line or Area  |  Yes, comparing periods → Grouped Bar  |  No → stay with category charts
How many categories?
2–8 → Bar/Column  |  8–15 → Horizontal Bar  |  15+ → Treemap or Table  |  Part-of-whole → Pie (max 6)
Do exact values matter?
Yes → Bar chart or Table (readers can compare precisely)  |  No → Pie, Treemap, Heatmap are fine
Who is the audience?
Executive → KPI Cards + Line + Bar only  |  Analyst → Scatter, Histogram, Heatmap OK  |  Mixed → default simple
What decision follows?
Operational → KPI cards with RAG status  |  Strategic → Trend + comparison  |  Exploratory → Scatter, Heatmap
Two variables to compare?
Both numeric → Scatter Plot  |  Cumulative change → Waterfall  |  One cat + one number → Bar
Magnitude AND composition?
Both matter → Stacked Bar (comparison) or Stacked Area (over time)
Section 03
8 Mistakes That Kill Dashboards

Every one of these makes evaluators cringe. Know them, avoid them.

3D Charts — Never
3D distorts proportions and makes data impossible to read accurately. There is zero valid reason to use 3D charts. Always use flat 2D.
Y-Axis Not Starting at Zero
Truncating the Y-axis makes tiny differences look huge. For bar charts, always start at zero. Line charts can be an exception, but be transparent.
Pie Chart With 10 Slices
Nobody can distinguish 10 wedges. If you have more than 6 categories, group small ones into "Other" or switch to a bar chart or treemap.
Chart Title Describes, Not Tells
"Bar Chart of Sales" = bad. "East Region Leads All Markets in Q1 2024" = good. A title should communicate the insight, not describe the chart type.
Too Many Colours
More than 6 colours = visual noise. Use one colour family. Highlight the key data point in a contrast colour. Grey out everything else.
KPI Without Context
"Revenue: ₹2.4Cr" tells nothing. "₹2.4Cr (+12% vs last month, 84% of target)" tells everything. A number alone is decoration, not information.
Dual Y-Axes
Two Y-axes on one chart confuse almost everyone. Instead of a combo chart, build two separate charts side by side. Simpler is always better.
Unlabelled Axes
Every axis needs a label and unit. "Sales" is not enough — "Sales (₹ Lakhs)" is correct. Every series needs a legend or direct label on the chart.

Sample Dashboard — Practical Example

One real business problem. Every chart type applied. Explained why each was chosen.

🎯 The Business Problem
Company: RetailCo India — an e-commerce company selling across 4 regions and 3 product categories.

Stakeholder ask: "Our VP of Sales wants a monthly dashboard to answer: How is the business performing overall? Which regions and products are driving growth? Where are we losing customers? How does this month compare to last year?"

Data available: 12 months of orders — Region, Category, Revenue, Orders, Returns, Customer Type, Date.
Row 1 — Top KPIs: What does the VP need to see first?
Total Revenue
₹4.8Cr
▲ +18% vs Last Year
Total Orders
12,840
▲ +11% vs Last Year
Target Achievement
87%
▼ Target: ₹5.5Cr
Return Rate
6.4%
▲ +1.2% vs Last Year
Why KPI Cards here?
The VP has 30 seconds to understand the business health before diving into detail. KPI cards deliver the single most important number per metric — with comparison context (vs target, vs last year). Without comparison, numbers mean nothing. This is always Row 1 of any executive dashboard.
Row 2 — Trend over time & Revenue mix
Monthly Revenue Trend
Jan 2024 – Dec 2024 · Electronics vs Clothing vs Home
Line Chart
Why a Line Chart?
The question is "how did revenue move over time?" Time is continuous → Line chart. Multiple lines = compare 3 categories across the same 12-month period. Seasonality (Q4 spike) is immediately visible.
Revenue by Category
Full year contribution to total ₹4.8Cr
Donut Chart
Why a Donut Chart?
The question is "what % of revenue does each category contribute?" That's a part-of-whole question with only 3 slices — perfect for a donut. The centre shows total revenue (₹4.8Cr) as a bonus KPI.
Row 3 — Regional performance & product comparison
Revenue by Region
Sorted highest to lowest — FY 2024
Bar Chart
Why a Bar Chart?
Comparing 4 discrete regions — classic bar chart scenario. Sorted descending so the eye immediately sees the ranking (South leads, West lags). Time is NOT involved here — it's a category comparison.
Category Revenue by Region
Electronics · Clothing · Home across all regions
Grouped Bar
Why a Grouped Bar?
Two dimensions simultaneously: Region (4 groups) AND Category (3 bars each). The VP can see not just which region is largest, but which category drives each region. South = Electronics dominant. West = weak across all.
Row 4 — Cumulative growth & correlation insight
Cumulative Revenue by Region Over Time
Monthly stacked — shows volume AND regional mix
Stacked Area
Why a Stacked Area?
Two questions at once: (1) How does total revenue grow over time? (2) How does each region's contribution shift month to month? Stacked area answers both simultaneously. South's area visibly growing = it's scaling faster.
Orders vs Revenue per Region-Month
Does more orders always = more revenue?
Scatter Plot
Why a Scatter Plot?
Correlation question: "Do regions with more orders generate more revenue?" Scatter reveals the relationship (and outliers). An outlier point = high orders but low revenue could mean returns, discounts, or low-ticket products — a key investigation lead.
Row 5 — Revenue bridge & order time patterns
Revenue Bridge: 2023 → 2024
What drove the ₹74L growth?
Waterfall
Why a Waterfall Chart?
The VP asks "we grew ₹74L — where did that come from?" Waterfall decomposes the growth: new customers added X, price increase added Y, returns cost Z. It's the go-to chart for any "explain the gap" question in finance or sales.
Orders by Day × Time of Day
When do customers actually buy?
Heatmap
Why a Heatmap?
Two categorical dimensions — Day of Week × Time of Day — and we need to spot the hot zones instantly. Colour intensity makes Friday 8–10pm (peak ordering time) obvious at a glance. A bar chart for this would need 56 bars — unreadable.
Dashboard Design Decisions — Summary
Row 1 — KPI Cards: Executive summary. One number per metric, always with context.
Row 2 — Line + Donut: Trend over time (line) + composition (donut). Two different angles on the same revenue data.
Row 3 — Bar + Grouped Bar: Regional ranking (bar) + two-dimension breakdown (grouped).
Row 4 — Area + Scatter: Cumulative growth view (area) + analytical correlation question (scatter).
Row 5 — Waterfall + Heatmap: Explain the change (waterfall) + reveal hidden time patterns (heatmap).
Key principle: Every chart answers one specific question. No chart is decorative.
