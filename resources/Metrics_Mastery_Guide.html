Module 01
What Is a Metric?

Before you can answer "which metric?", you need to know what a metric actually is — and what it is not.

Definition
Metric
A quantifiable measure used to track, assess, or evaluate the performance of a specific process, activity, or outcome over time.
Example: Daily Active Users (DAU), Revenue, Churn Rate
Definition
KPI
Key Performance Indicator — a metric that is directly tied to a strategic business objective. All KPIs are metrics, but not all metrics are KPIs.
Example: Monthly Revenue is a KPI. Page load time is a metric, not always a KPI.
Definition
Goal / Target
A specific value a metric should reach by a specific date. Goals give metrics meaning — without a target, a number is just a number.
Example: Goal = ₹10Cr revenue by Q4. Metric = Monthly Revenue. KPI = Revenue Growth %
Definition
North Star Metric
The single metric that best captures the core value your product delivers to customers. It aligns the entire company on one measurable outcome.
Example: Spotify's North Star = Time Spent Listening per User per Day
The Most Important Distinction

Vanity metrics look impressive but don't drive decisions. Actionable metrics tell you what to do next. An interviewer who asks "which metric?" is really asking: "do you know the difference?"

+ One More Category: Engagement

AARRR doesn't explicitly cover Engagement — but for consumer apps, it's the most watched metric category. Engagement metrics measure depth of usage: DAU/MAU ratio, session length, features used per session, notification open rate. A user can be "retained" (they came back) but not "engaged" (they did nothing).

Module 03
The North Star Metric

One metric to rule them all. Knowing a company's North Star — and why they chose it — shows deeper understanding than listing 10 random metrics.

What makes a good North Star?

It must measure the value delivered to customers (not just business outcomes), be actionable by most teams, correlate with long-term retention and revenue, and be specific enough to move — not something vague like "satisfaction".

Spotify
Time Spent Listening per User per Day
Captures whether users find music they love. High listening time predicts premium conversion and long-term retention.
Streaming
Netflix
Hours of Content Watched per Member per Month
Engaged viewers don't cancel. Hours watched predicts churn better than any satisfaction survey.
Streaming
Swiggy / Zomato
Orders Delivered per User per Month
Frequency of ordering = habit formation. A user who orders 4x/month is far less likely to churn than one who orders 1x.
Food-tech
WhatsApp
Messages Sent per Day (by active users)
WhatsApp's value = communication. The more messages sent, the stronger the network effect and the harder to leave.
Messaging
Airbnb
Nights Booked
Directly measures the core transaction — both sides of the marketplace (host + guest) succeed when nights are booked.
Marketplace
LinkedIn
Weekly Active Users who create or engage with content
Lurkers don't create network value. Content engagement drives the feed, job discovery, and recruiter tool value.
B2B SaaS
Uber
Trips Completed per Week
Every completed trip = revenue, driver earnings, and rider value. It captures both supply and demand side health.
Marketplace
Amazon
Number of Products Purchased per Customer per Year
Breadth of purchase = customer lifetime value. Prime members buy more categories = higher LTV and switching cost.
E-commerce
Interview Tip

When asked "what metric would you measure for [company]?", always start with the North Star, then break it into input metrics. Saying "I'd track DAU" without explaining how it connects to business value scores poorly. Saying "I'd track Orders per User because that predicts LTV and retention, and I'd break it into acquisition rate, conversion rate, and repeat order rate" scores very well.

Module 06
Metric Trees — Breaking Down a Metric

A metric tree shows how one top-level metric breaks into components. When a metric drops, you trace the tree to find the root cause.

How to use a metric tree in an interview: When a metric drops, never guess. Say "I'd break this metric into its components and check each one." This structured approach demonstrates analytical thinking. The tree below shows how Swiggy's North Star (Orders per User per Month) decomposes.
The "Why" Chain — Interview Technique

If DAU drops 15%, don't just say "DAU dropped." Walk the tree: Is it new users or returning users? → If returning: which cohort? → which platform (iOS/Android)? → which geography? → did anything change that day? (release, outage, competitor launch). This systematic decomposition is what separates a strong analyst from a weak one.
